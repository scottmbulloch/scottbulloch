The Net and Our Pets

Social networking websites, where you can write about your favorite things and show off your connections to friends, are some of the most popular applications on the web.

Millions of people each day log onto MySpace, Facebook and/or LinkedIn and check out their friends’ activities. Turns out, people aren’t the only ones who are networking. Our furry friends also have websites - Dogster and Catster.

These sites have been around for years, and they continue to grow by the hundreds of “critters” each day. They are operated by the same company and look remarkably similar, except, of course, one site features cats and the other one dogs.

Dogster and Catster allow people to share info on what’s unique and special about their pets. They can form groups according to breed, where they’re from, what they like to do, and their ailments. You can create a page for a dearly-departed pet and give him/her virtual wings.

When you get tired of networking with real people, Dogster and Catser are a nice walk in the park.

-Robyn

Time magazine warns against promotion of one aspect of a product as environmentally friendly while negative impacts are obscured. Or making claims that are vague or cannot be easily verified.

Buy One Give One

Speaking of Argentina, Texas Monthly’s Fall Fashion magazine has an article in it about Blake Mycoskie, a Texan who founded TOMS Shoes. While traveling in Argentina he said, “I met so many kids that did not have shoes. They walk for miles to school and to fetch water, and they develop nasty cuts and scrapes on their feet. I thought for a long time about how I could help them before having my epiphany to start TOMS.”

For every pair of shoes he sells, Mycoskie donates another pair to a needy child. So far, 60,000 pairs of shoes have been donated in South America and South Africa. They’re planning to donate another 200,000 pairs of shoes this year.

TOMS Shoes are canvas flats that come in a wide variety of styles, including a pair for people who can’t decide how they’ll vote in this year’s election.

In Austin, you can buy TOMS shoes at several locations, including Whole Earth Provision Company, Tyler’s, and Sola.

Sensible shoes for a good cause… something to make your feet and your heart happy.

Organization 101

Something about “back to school,” even if you don’t have little ones needing glue sticks and pencils, brings out the compulsive organizer in many of us.

Laura D. Sanders of Austin has some advice on getting your life in order:

Do you hate filing, file folders, and paper cuts? Do piles of incoming material grow on your desk, floor, or any flat, clean surface? You may be a "chronological filer," that is you instinctively keep papers in order by when they were received, rather than by subject or category.  Don't fight it! Spend a few dollars investing in an archival-quality cardboard box, set it up on end near your desk or other paper-accumulating spot, and prepare for painless document storage. 

You'll need a computer with spreadsheet software, or a pen or pencil and a notebook to use in creating an index.  Record the current date (not the date of the document) on one corner of the long side of the document, along with a time stamp, thusly:  "2008.08.08 16:37:00" (August 8th, 2008, 4:37pm in 24-hr notation). Record that same date/time stamp in your index, either computerized or handwritten, along with a description of the document and the box name (PC 1, for example, if you anticipate many boxes).

Place the document face-down (note, that's important), in the box.  Add new documents face-down on top of the previous one.  When the box is full, turn it to its normal position, and voila! The papers are all in numerical order and can be found in less than five minutes by consulting the index, then going to the proper spot in the box.  No file folders are needed. 

Just be sure to label the box and its cover.  You're in sync with your instincts, your frustration level is greatly reduced, and everyone else is impressed with your new-found efficiency and effectiveness.

If you don't feel you have the expertise to set up an index on your own, one helpful resource you can buy is Pile Cabinet, a software index system based on FileMaker Pro. It's inexpensive and definitely worth the money if you covered up in clutter.


Laura’s Advice for Bookworms

Does the traditional method of sorting things leave you cold? Are you never sure whether you want to “Keep," "Toss," or "Give Away," especially when it comes to collections like books, music, or art?  Try doing what works for you. 
For example, if you're an avid reader who often reads books more than once, and you want to whittle down your book collection, try making piles using these categories:

  1. "Read Many Times" (Keep!);

  2. "Read once, won't read again" (Give Away);

  3. "Read once, might read again" (Keep for six months in a labeled box, if not read, give away or sell); and

  4. "Enjoyed So Much I Want Others to Enjoy" (Give Away, perhaps to a special friend or someone you know would love the book).

Have some "Never Read" books in good shape and with ISBN numbers on the covers? Take those to your nearest used or Half-Price Books store and trade them in for something you KNOW you'll read many times! It's a great way to "re-use, re-cycle, re-store" while still enjoying your favorite pastime, and you might even earn some cash.

Where was I when cursive died? In fact, until last month, I didn’t even know it was ill. Cursive might not be completely dead, but it does have one foot in the grave and the other on a banana peeling, and a very slippery one at that.

I learned about cursive’s passing when I inscribed a book to my godchild on the occasion of her high school graduation.

As a writer, I penned what I hoped was a memorable note about how much it means to be her godmother, etc. It was just a few lines, but it took her forever to read it, and she even had to ask about one of the words.

Noticing my consternation, she said, “Sorry, Maggie, but I have a real hard time reading cursive.”

Now this is a girl who graduated in the Top 10 percent of her class, is fluent in German and has been accepted into a flagship university, yet she has trouble reading cursive! She explained that thanks to computers, text messaging and e-mails, writing is no longer necessary, and when she must write a thank you note, she prints.

Then I remembered the thank you notes I had received from other graduates, and none was in cursive. .

Curses! 2 bad. Beautifully looped Ls, perfectly circled Os, below the line Ps and carefully crossed Ts, will soon B-gone 4ever. U C what happens when U R’nt watching.
