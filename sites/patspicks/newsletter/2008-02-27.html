Super Delegates

It's super time!

If the February 5 primary elections and political caucuses were collectively known as "Super Tuesday," then Texas' and Ohio's primaries on March 4 should be dubbed "Super-duper Tuesday."

No presidential candidate has either party's nomination wrapped up, so it is super important that Texans go to the polls on March 4. Although not a certainty, it looks more and more like delegate-rich Texas and Ohio could decide both the Democratic and Republican presidential nominees.

Never mind that Rhode Island and Vermont are also holding primaries on March 4. Up against super-sized Texas and Ohio, pint-sized Rhode Island and Vermont have only a slim chance of attracting candidate or media attention.

In addition to super-voting days in this super-long campaign season, there are king-size spotlights shining on formerly little known about (at least outside the Democratic party) super delegates. These delegate's may pledge their votes to candidates, regardless of how their respective states or caucuses voted. So if most Texas Democrats opt for Hillary Clinton, but a bunch of the state's super politicos pledged their votes to Barack Obama, they don't have to change their stripes.

These super delegates make up about one-fifth of the delegates to the national convention, so if the Democratic race isn't over by convention time, you can bet that candidates will be super wooing both committed and uncommitted super-delegates.

Can't help but wonder if super delegates dress up like Superman so TV cameras and candidates can easily locate them, are they easily recognized by their super status, achievements and/or riches?

Republicans don't have designated super delegates. There are plenty of super-important folks in the GOP whose endorsements would be a super-prize for any candidate, but the party doesn't identify them by an over-used superlative.

One good thing about the super-hype about Super-duper Tuesday, is that Texas voters might realize how important it is to vote in the primary. Way too often, voters wait until the general election in November to cast their ballots because they mistakenly believe that this is when their votes count.

Super wrong! Because of partisan redistricting, except for statewide races, such as governor and U.S. senator, district-wide and local races are often decided in the primary election in March, rather than the general election in November.

So be super efficient and vote in the primary and in the general election. You will be super glad you did.

To learn more about the candidates, as though Texans haven't been super inundated with info already, log onto the League of Women Voters Web site: www.lwvtexas.org or to either party's Web site: www.texasgop.org or www.txdemocrats.org.

I don't know about you, but I will be super glad when the election is over, if for no other reason than super can be shelved for four years. Won't that be SUPER!
